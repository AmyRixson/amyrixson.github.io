Problem Solving

Problem solving is an incredibly important skill. It has manifested in many ways throughout my educational and professional journey; from solving equations, applying equations to models and solving challenging problems within code, to finding creative and effective ways to teach students during lockdown. I beleive that, through determination, I have learned to face challenges with resolve, patience and logic. I find that whatever problem I am faced with, I am now able to break them down into managagable steps in logical sequence - never forgetting the importance of seeking the advice of experts when available.

I first came about trying to improve my problem solving skills when completing my degree. At first thinking it was a purely mathematical skill. I found a list of advice and would follow it diligently - Defining Features of the set up, Leverage Symmetry, Visualising, Simplifying etc. However as I began coding, and finding problems to solve outside of pure maths, I learned that problem solving is as much about flexibility and thinking creatively as it is about thinking algorythmically. This realisation has helped me in my career as a teacher, whether solving the problem of how best to teach about graphs, how to make transformers sound exciting or in the behemoth problem of behaviour management - which has a different solution every day!

Communication

Working within a team, working with clients (or students and parents) necessarily requires excellent communication. It goes without saying the importance of clear, concise and kind communication within all environments. I pride myself on being able to communicate effectively. Working as a teacher I communicate with up to 150 students each day - delivering instruction, advice, guidence and feedback. I also work with teams of colleagues where we plan differnt aspacts of the curriculum and students' experience. I value advice and input from my teams and endeavour to make the best contribution that I can.

Reflective Practice

Being reflective in my practice as a teacher has been, perhaps one of the most valuable skills I could have learned. I now work on the basis of continuous growth (and try to encourage the same in my students). When an element of my practice doesn't work or isn't up to my standard there is a way to improve, always. I find Gibbs Reflective Cycle (learned in my PGCE) particularly useful. I look at situations with sequencial explaination and analysis and find a solution - then put into practice that solution and agin evaulate its effectiveness creating a cycle of growth.
